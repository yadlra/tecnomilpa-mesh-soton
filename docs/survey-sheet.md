## What's on it

The sheet has eight pages:

1. **Cover.** Surveyor metadata (name, date, pair, weather) and a short glossary of four words students will encounter on the walk: LoRa, Meshtastic, RSSI, Site Planner. The glossary is deliberately tease-not-cram: just enough to recognise the words when they appear, not a full vocabulary lesson.

2. **Pre-walk page.** A page for recording three predictions from the Site Planner before leaving October Books. See the [Site Planner walkthrough](site-planner.md) for the parameters used and the predictions we are testing.

3 to 6. **Four site pages**, one per candidate node location. Each has five observational blocks arranged in the order a person naturally encounters a place:

   - **Feel the place** (orientation, solar exposure, weather)
   - **Look up. Where could it go?** (antenna height, mounting affordance)
   - **Look toward the next site** (line of sight, clutter, link distance)
   - **Who's here, who owns it** (stewardship and permission)
   - **Verdict** (yes / no / maybe and one sentence why)

   Each block has a plain-language invitation in bold, two or three observational prompts, and a small green footer naming what radio engineers would call the thing the student has just noticed. The engineering vocabulary lands after the experience, not before.

7. **Decision page.** Filled in together at Highfield after the walk. Three boxes: the four chosen spots, the role each node would play (CLIENT or ROUTER), where the map and the walk disagreed, and what would still need checking before installation.

8. **Teacher's note.** A recommended teaching sequence, things to bring, and a paragraph on why embodied estimation (head height, first floor, second floor) is the right precision level for a speculative survey.

## Why this structure

Earlier drafts had eight to ten engineering criteria per site, each presented as a technical question. The problem with that approach is that it asks Year 2 art students to perform engineering expertise they have not been taught, which produces shallow answers. The shift to five observational blocks with vocabulary footers preserves all the engineering content but reorders the encounter: the body notices the place first, then the words arrive as names for what was noticed.

The pattern is reusable. The five-block structure (feel, look up, look toward, who's here, verdict) can be adapted for other site-survey work by swapping out the engineering vocabulary in the green footers. A community wifi survey, an accessibility audit, a sensor placement walk: all could use the same structure with different technical content in the footers.

## What to bring on the walk

For the teacher, who hands the sheets out:

- A printed route map with the four candidate sites marked
- Printed survey sheets, one per pair plus spares
- A reference node set up at October Books for taking comparison RSSI readings during the walk
- Phones with the Meshtastic app installed
- A tape measure (occasionally useful, mostly for show)
- Weather-appropriate clothes and a pen that works in the rain

The sheet itself is light. Students do not need anything beyond pen, sheet, phone, and a clipboard or hard surface.
